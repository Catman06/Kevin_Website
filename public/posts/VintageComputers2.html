After getting the Aptiva working, I felt the desire to do more with old computers. Looking around, I saw an old Dell tower that I was basically using as a bucket of old computer parts (I know it's not great to do that, but most of these weren't expected to ever be used again). I took a closer look at it and discovered that there were a few bulging capacitors, which wasn't too suprising, seeing as this computer is ~20 years old. At firse I thought that these capacitors were plagued, but after further research the computer came out just after the main years of the plague, so I'm not certain either way. Regardless, the solution remains the same: replace all the types of capacitors that have failed.

The process for recapping a motherboard is simple, first record where all the capacitors are and what they are. Fortunately Dell has identifiers for each capacitor silkscreened onto the board so I can just record that along with the capacitance and voltage. Next step is to find all the capacitors to be replaced. While I think you technically should replace all of the capacitors on the board, I decided to just go with the ones that had failed and ones that were of reportedly bad series.

After identifying the capacitors I wanted to replace I went to DigiKey to buy their replacements. I just found capacitors that were the same size, same voltage, same capacitance, and the best lifetime at at least the same temperature and ordered them. In order to do work on the motherboard, my current absolute bare-minimum soldering iron wouldn't do, so I also purchaced a slightly better than minumum soldering iron as well as a solder sucker. The new iron supported setting a temperature and had several interchangable tips, including a smaller one, which I needed to accurately desolder and solder the close together components on the motherboard more easily.

Once the new iron arrived, I set about desoldering all the replaced capacitors. While most went smoothly, some of the larger ones had massive areas of copper surrounding the hole, I think, which make heating the solder incredibly difficult. Ultimately, those solder I never really managed to fully get all of the solder out of the holes and just had to deal with that later.

With the old components out of the board, and the new capacitors ready to go in, I sat down to finish up the board. Similarly to when desoldering, most of the capacitors got soldered in without issue, but the large ones were hard to heat sufficiently and they still had solder clogging the hole. In order to get them in I just had to crank the iron very hot and melt the solder as I inserted the component. It didn't work well, but it did work, so despite the joints being quite poor, the capacitors were in place.

It was then time to actually test the board. I got the monitor and keyboard hooked up plugged it in and turned it on and it started giving a beep code. It did power on and nothing popped, so mostly a success for the recap it seemed. I looked up what the code meant, and it turned out to be an issue with memory. I had replaced a capacitor nearby the DIMM slots, so I was worried that something went wrong with that. I tried removing some memory to see if it was the memory that was bad, and the computer POSTed without issue. Relieved that I hadn't screwed anything up, I took the computer and set it up fully with what I believe is its original HDD. Just to make sure that the memory I had removed was really bad, I placed it back in and powered on and the computer POSTed just fine, so it apparently just needed to be re-seated or something.

Now that all the hardware was working, I wanted to get Windows XP back onto the computer, as that is what it would have shipped with. In fact it still had the XP badge and certificate of authenticity with the key for XP Home on it. Installation went smoothly, with me burning the XP Home installer onto a CD and then booting it. After installation I needed to find the necessary drivers for everything. Fortunately Dell still has all of the drivers and updates to drivers still available on their website, so I just downloaded all of them, stuck them on a flash drive and installed the ones that were needed for the Dell Dimension 4700. In addition to drivers, I upgraded Windows XP up to Service Pack 3.
